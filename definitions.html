Physics 1 Glossary

Key Definitions

Word-for-word definitions for every essential term — covering physical quantities, measurement quality, and all core mechanics concepts.

Quantity Physical Quantities
Physical Quantity A property of a phenomenon, body, or substance that can be quantified by measurement. Every physical quantity has a numerical value and a unit.
Scalar Quantity A quantity that is fully described by magnitude alone — no direction is needed. Examples: speed, mass, temperature, energy, distance, time.
Vector Quantity A quantity that requires both magnitude and direction for a complete description. Examples: displacement, velocity, acceleration, force, momentum.
Base Quantity One of the seven fundamental quantities from which all others are derived: length (m), mass (kg), time (s), electric current (A), thermodynamic temperature (K), amount of substance (mol), luminous intensity (cd).
Derived Quantity A quantity expressed as an algebraic combination of base quantities. Examples: velocity (m/s = m·s⁻¹), force (N = kg·m·s⁻²), energy (J = kg·m²·s⁻²).
SI Unit The International System of Units (Système International d'Unités) — the globally accepted standard measurement system built on seven base units.
Magnitude The numerical size of a quantity, always non-negative. The magnitude of a vector is its length: |v⃗| = v.
Dimension The expression of a quantity in terms of base quantities using symbols L (length), M (mass), T (time), etc. Used in dimensional analysis to check equations.
Quality & Measurement Precision & Accuracy
Accuracy The closeness of a measured value to the true (accepted) value. A measurement is accurate if systematic errors are small.
Precision The consistency and reproducibility of repeated measurements. Precise measurements cluster tightly together, regardless of whether they are close to the true value.
Uncertainty The quantitative range within which the true value is expected to lie, expressed as ± some value. Reflects limits of the measuring instrument and method.
Significant Figures The digits in a number that carry meaningful information about its precision. Non-zero digits are always significant; zeros depend on placement.
Systematic Error An error that is consistent and reproducible, causing measurements to be consistently too high or too low. Cannot be reduced by averaging; requires calibration or method correction.
Random Error An error caused by unpredictable fluctuations in the measurement process. Can be reduced by taking many measurements and averaging.
Resolution The smallest change in a quantity that a measuring instrument can detect. Limits the precision of any measurement made with that instrument.
Qualitative Description A description using words and categories rather than numbers. Example: "the ball moved quickly to the right."
Quantitative Description A description using numerical values and units. Example: "the ball moved 3.2 m to the right in 0.8 s."
Kinematics Motion
Position (x) The location of an object relative to a chosen reference point (origin) along a coordinate axis, expressed with sign to indicate direction.
Displacement (Δx) The change in position of an object: Δx = x_f − x_i. A vector quantity — direction and magnitude both matter. Not the same as distance.
Distance The total path length traveled by an object. A scalar quantity — always positive, independent of direction.
Speed The rate of change of distance with time. A scalar: speed = distance / time. Average speed = total distance / total time.
Velocity (v⃗) The rate of change of displacement with time. A vector: v⃗ = Δx⃗/Δt. Average velocity = displacement / time interval.
Instantaneous Velocity The velocity of an object at a single instant in time, equal to the slope of the position–time graph at that point: v = dx/dt.
Acceleration (a⃗) The rate of change of velocity with time: a⃗ = Δv⃗/Δt. A vector. Positive acceleration does not always mean speeding up — it depends on the sign of velocity.
Uniform Acceleration Acceleration that is constant in both magnitude and direction. The four kinematic equations apply only under this condition.
Projectile Motion Motion under gravity alone, where horizontal and vertical components are independent: aₓ = 0, aᵧ = −g. The path is parabolic.
Free Fall Motion in which gravity is the only force acting on an object. All freely falling objects near Earth's surface accelerate downward at g ≈ 9.8 m/s², regardless of mass.
Dynamics — Forces Newton's Laws
Force (F⃗) An interaction between two objects that causes, or tends to cause, a change in motion. A vector quantity measured in newtons (N = kg·m/s²).
Newton's Laws of Motion — 0th through 3rd
Newton's 0th Law The laws of physics are the same in all inertial (non-accelerating) reference frames — Galilean Relativity. This foundational principle makes Newton's other laws universally applicable within classical mechanics.
Newton's 1st Law An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion at constant velocity, unless acted upon by a net external force. Also called the Law of Inertia.
Newton's 2nd Law The net force on an object equals its mass times its acceleration: F⃗_net = ma⃗ = dp⃗/dt. The acceleration is in the same direction as the net force.
Newton's 3rd Law For every action force, there is an equal and opposite reaction force. Forces come in pairs that act on different objects; they never cancel each other on the same free-body diagram.
Inertia The tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion. Inertia is measured by mass — greater mass means greater inertia.
Mass (m) A measure of the quantity of matter in an object and its resistance to acceleration (inertia). A scalar, measured in kilograms (kg). Mass is not the same as weight.
Linear Momentum (p⃗) The product of mass and velocity: p⃗ = mv⃗. A vector. Newton's 2nd law in its general form states F⃗_net = dp⃗/dt — net force equals the rate of change of momentum.
Center of Mass The single point where the entire mass of a system can be treated as concentrated for the purpose of applying Newton's 2nd law. r⃗_cm = Σ(mᵢr⃗ᵢ) / Σmᵢ. Net external force equals total mass times acceleration of the center of mass.
Free-Body Diagram (FBD) A diagram showing only the object of interest and all forces acting on it. Each force is drawn as an arrow from the object's center. The essential first step in applying Newton's laws.
Weight (w) The gravitational force exerted on an object by a planet: w = mg. A vector directed downward. Unlike mass, weight varies with gravitational field strength.
Normal Force (N) The contact force exerted perpendicular to a surface on an object resting on or pressing against it. It is a reaction to the object pressing into the surface.
Friction A contact force that opposes relative motion or the tendency of motion between surfaces. Kinetic friction acts during sliding; static friction acts when surfaces are not moving relative to each other.
Tension (T) The pulling force transmitted through a string, rope, or cable. It acts along the rope, away from the object. For a massless rope, tension is equal throughout.
Net Force The vector sum of all forces acting on an object. Determines the object's acceleration. If net force is zero, the object is in equilibrium.
Equilibrium A state in which the net force (and net torque) on an object is zero. The object is either at rest (static equilibrium) or moving at constant velocity (dynamic equilibrium).
Energy & Work Conservation
Work (W) The energy transferred to or from an object by a force acting over a displacement: W = F·d·cosθ, where θ is the angle between F⃗ and d⃗. Measured in joules (J).
Kinetic Energy (KE) The energy an object possesses due to its motion: KE = ½mv². A scalar, always non-negative. Depends on mass and the square of speed.
Potential Energy (PE) Stored energy associated with an object's position or configuration within a force field. Gravitational PE: U_g = mgh; elastic PE: U_e = ½kx².
Mechanical Energy (E) The sum of kinetic and potential energy: E = KE + PE. Conserved when only conservative forces do work.
Work–Energy Theorem The net work done on an object equals the change in its kinetic energy: W_net = ΔKE. Applies even when forces are not constant.
Conservative Force A force for which the work done is independent of the path taken and depends only on the start and end points. Examples: gravity, spring force. Enables definition of potential energy.
Non-conservative Force A force for which the work done depends on the path taken. Examples: friction, air resistance. Converts mechanical energy into thermal energy.
Conservation of Energy The total energy of an isolated system remains constant. Energy can be transformed from one form to another but cannot be created or destroyed.
Power (P) The rate at which work is done or energy is transferred: P = W/t = Fv. Measured in watts (W = J/s).
Momentum & Impulse Collisions
Linear Momentum (p⃗) The product of an object's mass and velocity: p⃗ = mv⃗. A vector in the direction of velocity. Measured in kg·m/s.
Impulse (J⃗) The product of the net force and the time interval over which it acts: J⃗ = F⃗_net · Δt. Equal to the change in momentum: J⃗ = Δp⃗.
Impulse–Momentum Theorem The impulse applied to an object equals its change in momentum: J⃗ = Δp⃗ = m·Δv⃗. The vector form of Newton's second law integrated over time.
Conservation of Momentum The total momentum of an isolated system remains constant if no external net force acts on it: p⃗_total,i = p⃗_total,f.
Elastic Collision A collision in which both total kinetic energy and total momentum are conserved. Objects bounce off each other with no permanent deformation.
Inelastic Collision A collision in which total momentum is conserved but kinetic energy is not. Some KE is converted to heat, sound, or deformation energy.
Perfectly Inelastic Collision A collision in which the objects stick together after impact. Momentum is conserved; kinetic energy loss is the maximum possible for that collision.
Rotation & Circular Motion Torque & Angular
Angular Displacement (θ) The angle through which an object rotates. Measured in radians (rad). Positive by the right-hand rule (counterclockwise is positive).
Angular Velocity (ω) The rate of change of angular displacement: ω = Δθ/Δt. Measured in rad/s. Related to linear speed by v = rω.
Angular Acceleration (α) The rate of change of angular velocity: α = Δω/Δt. Measured in rad/s². Related to linear (tangential) acceleration by a_t = rα.
Torque (τ⃗) The rotational equivalent of force — a measure of a force's ability to cause angular acceleration: τ = rF sinθ, where r is the moment arm. Measured in N·m.
Moment of Inertia (I) The rotational equivalent of mass — a measure of an object's resistance to angular acceleration: I = Σmr². Depends on both mass and its distribution about the rotation axis.
Angular Momentum (L⃗) The rotational equivalent of linear momentum: L⃗ = Iω⃗. Conserved when net external torque is zero.
Centripetal Acceleration The acceleration directed toward the center of a circular path: a_c = v²/r = ω²r. It changes the direction of velocity, not its magnitude.
Centripetal Force The net inward force required to maintain circular motion: F_c = mv²/r. Not a new type of force — it is the net force directed toward the center (gravity, tension, normal force, etc.).
Moments of Mass 0th · 1st · 2nd
0th Moment of Mass The total mass of a system: M = ∫ dm. It is the "zeroth" moment because no positional weighting is applied — every mass element counts equally regardless of where it is.
1st Moment of Mass The mass-weighted sum of positions: ∫ r dm. Dividing by the total mass gives the center of mass: r_cm = (1/M) ∫ r dm — the balance point of the mass distribution.
2nd Moment of Mass The mass-weighted sum of squared distances from a rotation axis: I = ∫ r² dm. This is the moment of inertia — the rotational analog of mass, quantifying resistance to angular acceleration.
Why "Moments"? Each higher moment weights mass by an extra power of distance from a reference point. The 0th gives how much mass; the 1st gives where it is (center of mass); the 2nd gives how spread out it is (moment of inertia).
Gravity & Simple Harmonic Motion SHM & Oscillations
Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation Every two masses attract each other with a force proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them: F_g = Gm₁m₂/r².
Gravitational Field (g⃗) The gravitational force per unit mass at a point in space: g⃗ = F⃗_g/m. Near Earth's surface g ≈ 9.8 m/s² directed downward. It is the acceleration of free fall.
Gravitational Potential Energy The energy stored in the gravitational interaction between two masses. Near Earth's surface: U_g = mgh (taking h = 0 as reference). General form: U_g = −Gm₁m₂/r.
Orbital Velocity The speed required for a circular orbit at radius r from a planet of mass M: v = √(GM/r). Gravity provides the centripetal force.
Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) Oscillatory motion in which the restoring force is proportional to and directed opposite to the displacement: F = −kx. The motion is sinusoidal in time.
Amplitude (A) The maximum displacement from equilibrium in SHM. It determines the energy of the oscillation but not its period or frequency.
Period (T) The time for one complete oscillation. For a mass-spring: T = 2π√(m/k); for a simple pendulum: T = 2π√(L/g). Independent of amplitude (for small oscillations).
Frequency (f) The number of complete oscillations per unit time: f = 1/T. Measured in hertz (Hz = s⁻¹).
Restoring Force The force that acts to return a system to its equilibrium position. Its magnitude is proportional to displacement, and it always points toward equilibrium.
