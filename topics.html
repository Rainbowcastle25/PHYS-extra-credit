Concept Summaries

Physics 1 Core Topics

Eight units in order of dependency. Click any topic to expand its concept summary, key ideas, and common exam traps.

01
Kinematics
Motion without causes · Vectors · Projectiles
▾

Kinematics describes how objects move without asking why. The four kinematic equations apply only when acceleration is constant. Always define a positive direction before writing equations.

Key concepts:

Position (x), displacement (Δx), distance — displacement is a vector, distance is not.
Average velocity = Δx/Δt; instantaneous velocity = slope of x–t graph.
Acceleration = Δv/Δt; slope of v–t graph. Area under v–t = displacement.
Projectile motion: horizontal (aₓ = 0) and vertical (aᵧ = −g) are independent.
At max height of a projectile: vᵧ = 0, but vₓ ≠ 0.
Range is maximized at θ = 45° (on flat ground).

Common traps: Forgetting to split v₀ into components; using g = +9.8 when you set downward as negative.

02
Newton's Laws & Dynamics
Forces · Free-body diagrams · Friction
▾

Newton's three laws govern how forces cause changes in motion. Always start with a free-body diagram (FBD) — label every force acting on the object, not forces the object exerts on others.

Key concepts:

1st Law (Inertia): an object at rest/constant velocity has F_net = 0.
2nd Law: F_net = ma. Net force causes acceleration in the same direction.
3rd Law: forces come in equal-and-opposite action–reaction pairs on different objects.
Normal force (N) is perpendicular to the surface; it is NOT always equal to mg.
Kinetic friction: f_k = μ_k N (opposes motion). Static friction: f_s ≤ μ_s N.
On inclines: break weight into components parallel and perpendicular to slope.

Common traps: Including reaction forces on the same FBD; forgetting tension is the same throughout a massless rope.

03
Work, Energy & Power
Work–Energy Theorem · Conservation of Energy
▾

Energy is the most powerful tool in Physics 1. When only conservative forces act, total mechanical energy is conserved: KE + PE = constant. This often lets you skip Newton's laws entirely.

Key concepts:

Work = F·d·cosθ — only the component of force along displacement does work.
Work–Energy Theorem: W_net = ΔKE. All net work goes into changing KE.
Gravitational PE = mgh (reference point is your choice). Spring PE = ½kx².
Conservative forces (gravity, spring) have path-independent work. Friction is non-conservative.
With friction: KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f + W_friction (energy lost to heat).
Power = W/t = Fv (watts = J/s).

Common traps: Forgetting cosθ in the work formula; using PE = mgh when the reference height is ambiguous.

04
Momentum & Collisions
Impulse · Elastic · Inelastic
▾

Momentum is always conserved when the net external force is zero. Identify the system: if all colliding objects are in the system, internal forces cancel and total momentum is conserved.

Key concepts:

Momentum: p = mv (vector). Impulse: J = FΔt = Δp.
Conservation: m₁v₁ᵢ + m₂v₂ᵢ = m₁v₁f + m₂v₂f (when F_ext = 0).
Elastic collision: KE conserved AND momentum conserved.
Perfectly inelastic: objects stick together. Momentum conserved, KE not.
Center of mass: x_cm = (m₁x₁ + m₂x₂)/(m₁+m₂). It moves at constant v if F_ext = 0.

Common traps: Assuming KE is conserved in all collisions. It only is in elastic ones.

05
Circular Motion & Gravitation
Centripetal force · Orbits · Kepler's laws
▾

An object in circular motion accelerates toward the center even at constant speed — its direction changes. The centripetal force is not a new force; it is provided by existing forces (gravity, tension, normal force, friction).

Key concepts:

Centripetal acceleration: a_c = v²/r (directed toward center).
Centripetal force: F_c = mv²/r. Ask: what supplies this force in the problem?
Period and frequency: T = 2πr/v; f = 1/T.
Universal gravity: F_g = Gm₁m₂/r² (inverse-square law).
For circular orbit: gravitational force = centripetal force → v = √(GM/r).
Kepler's 3rd law: T² ∝ r³.

Common traps: Treating centripetal force as an extra force on FBDs. It is the net inward force.

06
Rotation & Torque
Angular kinematics · Moment of inertia · Rolling
▾

Rotational mechanics is the angular analog of linear mechanics. Every linear quantity (x, v, a, F, m) has a rotational counterpart (θ, ω, α, τ, I). The equations have the same form.

Key concepts:

Angular kinematics: same four equations with θ, ω, α replacing x, v, a.
Torque: τ = rF sinθ. Positive = counterclockwise (convention).
Newton's 2nd for rotation: τ_net = Iα.
Moment of inertia depends on shape AND axis. Disk: I = ½MR². Hoop: I = MR².
Angular momentum: L = Iω. Conserved when τ_net = 0.
Rolling without slipping: v_cm = Rω. Total KE = ½mv² + ½Iω².

Common traps: Using the wrong moment of inertia formula for the axis; forgetting the parallel-axis theorem.

07
Simple Harmonic Motion
Springs · Pendulums · Energy in SHM
▾

SHM occurs whenever the restoring force is proportional to displacement: F = −kx. The motion is sinusoidal. Period does NOT depend on amplitude (for small angles in a pendulum).

Key concepts:

x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ) — amplitude A, angular frequency ω, phase φ.
ω = 2πf = 2π/T. For spring: ω = √(k/m). For pendulum: ω = √(g/L).
Velocity is maximum at equilibrium (x = 0); zero at extremes (x = ±A).
Acceleration is maximum at extremes; zero at equilibrium.
Energy: E = ½kA² = ½mv²_max. KE and PE trade off; total E is constant.
Pendulum: T = 2π√(L/g). Period depends on L and g, not mass or amplitude.

Common traps: Thinking heavier masses oscillate slower on a spring. Heavier = slower. Pendulum mass doesn't matter.

08
Gravitation
Universal law · Gravitational PE · Escape velocity
▾

Newton's law of universal gravitation describes the force between any two masses. Near Earth's surface, g ≈ 9.8 m/s² is a simplification; the full law uses F = Gm₁m₂/r².

Key concepts:

F_g = Gm₁m₂/r² — decreases with the square of distance.
Gravitational PE = −GMm/r (negative! zero is at infinity).
Escape velocity: v_esc = √(2GM/r) — set KE = |PE| and solve.
For circular orbits: GMm/r² = mv²/r → v_orbit = √(GM/r).
Kepler's 1st: orbits are ellipses. 2nd: equal areas in equal times. 3rd: T² ∝ r³.

Common traps: Using PE = mgh (only valid near Earth's surface). Use −GMm/r for general cases.
